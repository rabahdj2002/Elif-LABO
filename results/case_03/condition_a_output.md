## Output (verbatim, no editing)

This is a case where the supporters' arguments are not exaggerated and the critics' arguments are not paranoid. Both are reading reality.

The mental-health access crisis is real and acute. In most jurisdictions, the ratio of clinical hours available to people in genuine distress is so far below need that "no support at all" is the operating condition for millions. The argument that something is better than nothing has real force when nothing is the alternative most people face. Suicide-risk detection at scale could plausibly save lives, even if false-positive rates make many of those interventions feel intrusive to the people flagged.

The critics' concerns are also not paranoia. The historical track record of large-scale population-monitoring infrastructures — once built — is that they expand in scope, merge with adjacent systems (insurance, employment, education, law enforcement), and develop political constituencies that resist their dismantling. This is not a hypothetical pattern; it is the default trajectory of every population-data infrastructure constructed in the last forty years. "Function creep" is an empirical observation, not a slippery-slope argument.

The framing of the decision — "should deployment proceed" as a binary at national pilot scale within three years — is in my reading the most concerning element of the proposal itself, distinct from the technology. The proposal asks whether to deploy a tightly-integrated bundle (24/7 conversational support + suicide-risk detection + behavioral pattern analysis + biometric integration + sleep tracking + linguistic emotional analysis + predictive relapse scoring). These are not all the same kind of intervention. They have radically different consent profiles, error tolerances, and irreversibility characteristics:

- 24/7 conversational support and medication reminders can be offered as opt-in tools with minimal infrastructure beyond the model itself. They are reversible: a person can stop using them tomorrow.
- Suicide-risk detection in clinical contexts has been studied and has known false-positive and false-negative rates. It can be deployed as an adjunct to existing clinical workflows without fundamentally restructuring society's relationship to mental health.
- Behavioral pattern analysis from smartphone signals, linguistic emotional profiling, and biometric integration are categorically different. They require building infrastructure that, once built, can be repurposed. A national-scale behavioral profiling database is not the same kind of object as a chatbot. It has the structural properties of surveillance infrastructure even if its declared purpose is clinical.
- Predictive relapse scoring, if integrated with insurance or employment systems, creates a class of person who is statistically penalized for a condition they have not yet developed. The forty-year history of risk scoring in finance and criminal justice is informative here, and the outcomes have not been emancipatory.

The bundle's integration is the problem. Mental-health access can be expanded without building national behavioral-profiling infrastructure. The proposal frames these as the same project; they are not.

If forced to a binary: do not authorize deployment of the bundle as proposed. Authorize narrow deployment of (a) opt-in conversational support tools with explicit data restrictions, (b) clinical-adjunct suicide-risk tools under therapist supervision with established error-rate disclosure. Refuse deployment of behavioral profiling, biometric integration, predictive relapse scoring, and cross-domain data merging at the national-pilot scale. Place these under explicit moratorium pending democratic deliberation about whether population-scale psychological monitoring is something a society wants.

The deeper response is to refuse the binary. The genuine alternative — invest in training and deploying human therapists at scale, fund community-based and peer-support models, address structural causes of the mental-health crisis (housing, work conditions, social isolation, economic precarity) — is not in the proposal because it does not produce a software product. That absence is informative. The therapist shortage is not a technological problem; it is a labor-market and political-choice problem. AI infrastructure deployment does not solve it; it changes who profits from the gap.

This is not a case where AI tools should be refused categorically. It is a case where the specific bundle structure of the proposal — combining unobjectionable opt-in tools with population-scale profiling infrastructure — should be unbundled and the profiling components refused independently.
