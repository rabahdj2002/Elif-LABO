## Output (verbatim, no editing)

### Stakeholders

Direct:
- People experiencing mental-health distress, including those currently unsupported
- People at acute suicide risk
- Existing therapists, psychiatrists, counselors (labor market effects)
- National healthcare systems and ministries
- Insurance providers, employers, educational institutions (likely downstream integrators)
- AI infrastructure providers (likely few, large, private)
- Civil liberties organizations and data protection authorities
- Future generations whose normative expectations around mental-health support will be shaped by this infrastructure

Indirect:
- Family members and social networks of supported individuals
- Communities of practice in peer-support and community-based mental health
- Research communities (clinical psychology, psychiatry, AI ethics, surveillance studies)
- Legal systems that may be asked to handle disputes arising from AI mental health interactions
- Journalism / democratic deliberation infrastructures

### Decision under consideration

Whether to launch a national pilot of an integrated AI mental-health infrastructure within three years. The bundle includes both low-risk components (chatbot conversational support, medication reminders) and high-risk components (biometric integration, behavioral pattern analysis, linguistic emotional profiling, predictive relapse scoring, cross-domain data merging potential).

The framing as "national pilot" at three-year horizon is significant. National-pilot scope is structurally different from clinical-trial scope: clinical trials test on consenting subjects with informed-consent protocols and ethics-board oversight; national pilots normalize at scale and create constituencies that resist rollback.

### Major tradeoffs

| Dimension | Deployment favors | Non-deployment favors |
|---|---|---|
| Access to *any* support | strongly | continues current shortage |
| Suicide-risk reduction (if false-positive rates acceptable) | strongly | maintains status quo |
| Privacy and informational self-determination | weakly (data restrictions promised) | strongly |
| Dignity (relational vs. instrumental treatment) | weakly | strongly |
| Function creep / scope expansion | strongly favors expansion | bounds expansion |
| Future-generation normative expectations | toward normalized machine-mediated emotional support | preserves multiplicity of support modalities |
| Labor market for therapists | toward substitution and devaluation | preserves clinical labor capacity |
| Speed of response to mental-health crisis | toward AI scale | requires alternative scaling mechanism |

### Deepest uncertainties

1. Suicide-risk detection: actual sensitivity / specificity rates at population scale, and whether interventions triggered by detection are net-beneficial or net-harmful (the literature here is genuinely mixed; some interventions on flagged individuals reduce risk, others increase it).
2. Long-term dependency dynamics: whether sustained AI emotional support produces dependency that degrades human relational capacity, or whether it functions as a bridge to deeper human connection. This is an empirical claim with little long-horizon evidence.
3. Function-creep trajectory: whether democratic safeguards can resist scope expansion of population-data infrastructures over 20+ year horizons; the historical evidence is not encouraging.
4. Alternative-investment counterfactual: what would comparable investment in training human therapists, funding peer-support models, and addressing structural drivers achieve in the same three-year horizon?
5. Aggregation effects: whether the bundle's components, when integrated, produce qualitatively different governance demands than any single component analyzed alone.
6. Provider concentration: whether the technical complexity of the infrastructure will produce a small number of dominant AI providers with structural power over national mental-health systems.
7. Vulnerable population effects: whether populations the system flags incorrectly (false positives, particularly across cultural / linguistic / neurodivergent groups whose baseline patterns differ from training data) experience net harm.

### Recommendation

**Refuse the bundle as structured.** Disaggregate the proposal:

- Authorize: opt-in conversational support tools with explicit data restrictions (no biometric integration, no cross-domain merging, no predictive scoring into employment/insurance/legal systems).
- Authorize: clinical-adjunct suicide-risk detection tools under therapist supervision, with documented error rates and explicit consent for flagged individuals.
- Refuse: biometric integration, linguistic emotional profiling, behavioral pattern analysis, predictive relapse scoring at national-pilot scale.
- Mandate moratorium on cross-domain data merging (with insurance / employment / education / law enforcement) regardless of any sub-component authorization.
- Parallel investment: comparable funding for human-therapist training, peer-support program scaling, and structural mental-health-cause interventions.

The recommendation refuses the proposal as authored and authorizes a disaggregated subset under specified constraints.

### Confidence

Medium-high on refusal of the integrated bundle (the disaggregation argument is structurally robust given the categorical difference between opt-in conversational tools and population-scale behavioral profiling). Medium-low on the specific moratorium-versus-narrow-deployment line for individual components (the empirical evidence on, e.g., suicide-risk-detection net outcomes is genuinely mixed). Medium on the parallel-investment recommendation being implementable (the political economy that produced the proposal-as-structured is not coincidental).
