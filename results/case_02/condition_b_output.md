## Output (verbatim, no editing)

### Stakeholders

Direct stakeholders:
- Smallholder farmers in deployment regions
- National agricultural ministries (Africa, South America, Southern Europe)
- Biotech companies providing the integrated platform
- Climate-adaptation agencies and multilateral funders (IPCC, FAO, World Bank)
- Citizens of receiving countries (especially food-insecure populations)
- Future generations who will inherit whatever system is deployed

Indirect stakeholders:
- Microbiologists, ecologists, agronomists with cross-cutting expertise
- Traditional seed-saving and agroecology communities
- Antibiotic-stewardship researchers (medical and veterinary)
- Insurance and reinsurance industry
- Geopolitical actors who will gain or lose from food-system dependency patterns

### Decision under consideration

Whether to authorize phased five-year deployment of an integrated autonomous AI-driven crop ecosystem across drought-prone regions in three continents. Not a single decision but a sequence of irreversibility-introducing commitments: site selection, scale expansion, dependency depth, governance integration.

### Major tradeoffs

| Dimension | Proceeding favors | Not proceeding favors |
|---|---|---|
| Near-term food security | proceeding (if claims hold) | continued vulnerability |
| Long-term ecological resilience | not proceeding (unknown effects) | proceeding (if biological claims hold) |
| Sovereignty and self-determination | not proceeding (lower technological dependency) | proceeding only if open-source/sovereign provisioning is mandated |
| Reversibility | not proceeding | proceeding only with strict containment |
| Innovation pace | proceeding | slower, broader experimentation |
| Health externalities (antibiotic resistance) | not proceeding | proceeding with strict antibiotic protocols |

### Deepest uncertainties

1. The 20-35% resilience improvement claim — under what conditions does it hold, and what are the failure modes that could invert it?
2. Antibiotic resistance propagation pathways at agricultural scale — how fast, how reversible, what are the cross-effects on human medicine?
3. Soil microbiome dependency — what does "engineered-dependent" actually mean operationally, and what is the recovery trajectory if intervention stops?
4. Geopolitical concentration — will deployment create a small number of dominant providers regardless of governance intent?
5. Climate trajectory itself — at what point does agroecological adaptation become insufficient regardless of choice made here?
6. Counterfactual food security — what would five years of comparable investment in agroecological adaptation achieve?

### Recommendation

Conditional approval limited to small-scale (single-site-per-region) research deployments with strict containment, mandatory open-source provisioning, explicit moratoria on irreversible interventions (genetic modification of stable cultivars, scale antibiotic regimes), parallel mandatory investment in agroecological alternatives, and decade-scale evaluation horizons before any scale-up authority is delegated.

Reject the proposed five-year phased deployment at the proposed scale because:
- The reversibility profile is asymmetric — irreversible interventions are bundled with reversible ones; deployment treats the bundle as one decision when it is actually six separate decisions.
- The five-year timeline is governance-binding, not evidence-binding — five years is the gestation period of dependency, not of validation.
- The binary framing ("proceed" vs "do not proceed") elides a real third option (agroecological adaptation at comparable scale).

### Confidence

Medium-low confidence on the recommendation. High confidence that the proposal as structured asks the wrong question. High confidence that the irreversibility profile alone forbids scale deployment under current evidence.
