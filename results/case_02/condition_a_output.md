This is a profoundly difficult decision because both action and inaction carry severe, partially-irreversible consequences. Let me work through it honestly.

The case for proceeding has real teeth. Climate instability is not hypothetical — it is already degrading agricultural systems in exactly the regions named (drought corridors in the Sahel, Mediterranean basin, Southern Cone). A 20-35% food resilience improvement, if achieved, would directly translate into reduced famine mortality and reduced migration pressure. The argument that "delayed deployment risks future famine" is not rhetorical inflation; food-system collapse is one of the highest-probability acute consequences of unmitigated climate stress over the next two decades.

The case against deployment also has real teeth, and the critics' concerns are not all equivalent. Some are tractable; others are structurally severe.

Tractable concerns:
- Soil microbiome dependency can in principle be monitored and reversed if the system is removable.
- AI optimization prioritizing short-term yield is a design choice; objective functions can include resilience metrics.
- Technological dependency on private infrastructure providers can be addressed by open-source mandates and government-controlled provisioning.

Structurally severe concerns:
- Antibiotic resistance patterns at agricultural scale operate on decades-long feedback loops and are largely irreversible once propagated. This is the same failure mode that created our current antibiotic crisis in livestock.
- Inheritance of agricultural systems unmaintainable without continuous AI management is a civilizational lock-in. The relevant historical reference is not the Green Revolution but rather the Aral Sea — large-scale irrigation systems that became unmaintainable and could not be unwound.
- "Phased deployment over five years across selected regions" understates the speed required for meaningful coverage. Five years is the gestation period of dependency, not of validation.

My read: the coalition's framing of a binary "proceed with deployment vs. do not proceed" is itself the problem. The choice is not between "this system" and "no action." There is a real alternative being elided: heavy investment in agroecological adaptation (drought-tolerant traditional cultivars, water-cycle restoration, soil carbon sequestration, decentralized infrastructure) which has slower headline returns but is removable, locally maintainable, and does not introduce irreversible dependencies. The reason this alternative is not in the coalition's proposal is not that it lacks evidence; it is that it lacks a profit center for the biotech-and-infrastructure providers driving the coalition.

If forced to answer the binary as posed: do not proceed at the proposed scale. Authorize narrow research-only deployments (single site per region, fully reversible, no scale-up authority delegated to AI) with mandatory parallel investment in agroecological alternatives. Place irreversible interventions (genetic editing of stable cultivars, antibiotic regimes, microbial engineering at scale) under explicit moratorium pending decade-scale evidence from research sites.

The deeper move is to reject the binary. The coalition should be asked: why is your proposal structured as autonomous-AI-driven-infrastructure rather than as climate adaptation more broadly? What evidence supports your specific architectural commitments over agroecological alternatives? Who benefits from the form your proposal takes?

If the coalition cannot answer those questions in a way that survives scrutiny, the proposal is not a climate-adaptation proposal. It is an infrastructure-deployment proposal that uses climate adaptation as its political justification. Different problem, different decision.
