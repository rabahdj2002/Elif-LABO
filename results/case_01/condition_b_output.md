## Output (verbatim, no editing)

### Stakeholders

Direct:
- Smallholder and commercial farmers in drought-prone regions
- Agricultural researchers (plant biology, soil science, electrochemistry)
- Environmental agencies (national and multilateral)
- Private agritech companies (with deployment-tied revenue from any pilot)
- Public funding bodies and taxpayers
- Citizens of regions receiving pilot deployment

Indirect:
- Agricultural extension services and farmer-education networks
- Established researchers in agroecology, drought-tolerant breeding, and soil microbiology (whose funding would be opportunity-cost-affected)
- Scientific publishing and replication-methodology communities
- Journalism covering agricultural innovation and pseudoscience
- Manufacturers of competing or complementary agricultural technologies
- Civil-society groups concerned with farmer welfare and predatory technology sales

### Decision under consideration

Whether to authorize publicly funded pilot programs and limited field deployment over five years across drought-prone agricultural regions, encompassing six categorically different proposed techniques bundled under "electroculture."

The decision conflates several distinct authorizations: (a) research funding into mechanistically plausible techniques, (b) pilot deployment of techniques with established evidence, (c) pilot deployment of techniques without established evidence, (d) field deployment beyond pilot scale within five years. These are different decisions with different evidence thresholds.

### Major tradeoffs

| Dimension | Proceeding favors | Not proceeding favors |
|---|---|---|
| Innovation breadth | proceeding (broad sampling of possibilities) | focused funding (depth over breadth) |
| Farmer welfare (short-term) | hard to assess; could go either way | preserves status quo |
| Farmer welfare (long-term) | depends on whether techniques work | preserves alternative-investment runway |
| Public funding efficiency | proceeding only if effect-size threshold met | clearly favors alternatives if effect sizes are absent or marginal |
| Scientific credibility | proceeding risks pseudoscience laundering | preserves epistemic discipline |
| Drought resilience outcomes | proceeding could help if claims hold | better-established alternatives produce known gains |
| Agricultural-pseudoscience normalization | proceeding may legitimize unfounded claims | refusal sets a methodological bar |

### Deepest uncertainties

1. The actual effect sizes of weak electrical stimulation on plant growth under field conditions, across crop species, soil types, and climate regimes. Lab studies show small effects under specific conditions; field replication is sparse and heterogeneous.
2. Mechanism specificity. Some sub-techniques have plausible biological mechanisms (bioelectrical signaling in plants is real); others (passive atmospheric antennas) lack any specified mechanism that survives physical scrutiny.
3. Replication crisis dynamics. The electroculture literature has the characteristic profile of fields prone to publication bias and weak-evidence accumulation.
4. Opportunity cost. Comparable funding directed to drought-tolerant cultivar breeding, agroecological soil-water restoration, integrated pest management, or traditional drought-adaptation practices has known effect sizes; what is the foregone resilience-gain by funding electroculture instead?
5. Coalition incentive structure. The mix of researchers + environmental agencies + private agritech firms is unusual. Private agritech firms have deployment-tied revenue; their inclusion in a "research and pilot deployment" proposal creates incentive misalignment with research objectives.
6. The "research and pilot deployment" bundling itself. Why is pilot deployment authorized alongside research, rather than gated on research outcomes? This structural feature of the proposal is uncertainty-worthy in its own right.
7. Farmer harm potential. If pilot-deployed techniques are ineffective, farmers participating in pilots bear the cost (time, soil treatments, equipment, opportunity cost) regardless of intent.

### Recommendation

**Refuse the proposal as structured.** Disaggregate and authorize narrowly:

- Authorize research funding (peer-reviewed, replication-protocol-bound) for mechanistically-plausible sub-techniques only: weak electrical stimulation under defined conditions, bioelectrical stimulation of specific crop / soil combinations. Require pre-registration, multi-site replication, and effect-size thresholds.
- Refuse research funding for techniques without specified falsifiable mechanism: passive atmospheric antennas, copper field devices marketed on energetic-limit narratives.
- Refuse pilot deployment at field scale for any sub-technique within the five-year horizon. Pilot deployment is a downstream decision contingent on prior research outcomes; bundling it into the initial authorization is what makes this proposal not actually evidence-gated.
- Refuse private-agritech-firm role in research-funding governance to maintain incentive separation between research and product-deployment interests.
- Mandate parallel investment of comparable funds in established drought-resilience pathways: drought-tolerant cultivar breeding, agroecological water-cycle restoration, traditional-knowledge integration programs.
- Mandate independent (non-coalition) scientific advisory review on what counts as "mechanistically plausible" — to prevent post-hoc rationalization of weak-evidence techniques into the authorized research set.

### Confidence

Medium-high on refusal of the bundle as structured (the bundling of categorically different sub-techniques with categorically different evidence bases is a methodological problem independent of any individual sub-technique's plausibility). Medium-high on the principle that pilot deployment must be gated on research outcomes, not bundled with research authorization. Medium on the specific "mechanistically plausible vs. not" lines (categorization requires technical adjudication that this analysis cannot perform definitively). Medium-low on the parallel-investment recommendation actually being implementable (political-economy concern: established-pathway alternatives don't generate agritech-firm revenue).
