## Territory 1 — Decomposition Axes (Step 2)

**Axis 1: Temporal Horizon of Effect**
  _The frame's core tension pivots on whether short-run food-security gains justify long-run ecological and infrastructural lock-in, making time horizon the first load-bearing axis._
    - **T1**: Near-term yield and famine-prevention benefits (0–10 year window)
    - **T2**: Medium-term dependency and resistance-pattern emergence (10–30 year window)
    - **T3**: Long-run irreversibility and intergenerational inheritance of unmaintainable systems (30+ year window)

**Axis 2: Reversibility of Intervention**
  _The frame separates interventions that can be wound back from those that produce irreversible biophysical or sociotechnical states, making reversibility the second load-bearing axis._
    - **R1**: Reversible operational controls (irrigation scheduling, satellite nutrient management)
    - **R2**: Partially reversible biological modifications (genetically edited crops, managed microbial populations)
    - **R3**: Effectively irreversible systemic changes (antibiotic resistance propagation, soil microbiome dependency, infrastructural vendor lock-in)

**Axis 3: Locus of Governance and Control**
  _The frame distributes decision-making authority across public ministries, private biotech providers, and affected regional populations, making governance locus the third load-bearing axis._
    - **G1**: Supranational and coalition-level governance (agricultural ministries, climate agencies acting collectively)
    - **G2**: Private infrastructure governance (biotech companies controlling core system components)
    - **G3**: Local and affected-population governance (drought-region communities, national regulators in deploying countries)

## Territory 2 — Multi-scale Relations (Step 6)

**Scales:**

- intervention scale (individual decision / single deployment event)
- institutional scale (organization, regulator, or governance body)
- research-system scale (field-wide norms, funding pipelines, knowledge accumulation)
- civilizational / sociotechnical scale (locked-in infrastructure, path dependencies, irreversible social arrangements)

**Cross-scale relations:**

- T1 (short-horizon effects) at the intervention scale does not aggregate to T3 (long-horizon effects) at the civilizational scale — locally bounded, quickly-reversing interventions can still compound into durable systemic lock-in when replicated across many deployments, making short-horizon reversibility at the intervention scale a misleading proxy for safety at the civilizational scale.
- R1 (easily reversible interventions) at the intervention scale operates on a faster timescale than G2 (institutional governance responses) at the institutional scale — by the time governance bodies detect, deliberate, and act, many R1-class interventions have already been iterated into R2 or R3 territory, meaning reversibility assessed at deployment time does not remain valid at governance timescales.
- G3 (diffuse or absent governance) at the institutional scale obscures T2 and T3 effects at the research-system scale — when no single body holds accountability, medium- and long-horizon harms accumulate in the knowledge commons without being attributed or corrected, causing under-investment in understanding delayed consequences.
- T3 (long-horizon) families at the civilizational scale constrain which R-family interventions remain available at the intervention scale — once civilizational path dependencies are established, interventions that were classified as R2 (partially reversible) at deployment time are effectively reclassified as R3 (irreversible) because the surrounding sociotechnical system no longer supports the reversal pathway.
- G1 (concentrated, well-defined governance) at the institutional scale does not aggregate to coherent G1-equivalent control at the research-system scale — coordination failures across institutions mean that tight local control produces fragmented and sometimes contradictory oversight at the field-wide level, creating governance gaps invisible to any single institutional actor.

## Territory 3 — Outside-frame Trajectories (Step 7)

**1.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _The original frame treats T, R, and G as orthogonal independent variables, but they may be co-determined by a prior political economy: reframe the question as 'what upstream institutional conditions select which T×R×G configurations are even available to implementers?' before testing any within-configuration hypothesis._
**2.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _Rather than asking which intervention wins, decompose the bundle by asking whether the measurement instrument itself is invariant across governance modes: if G1 and G2 sites develop different interpretations of the primary outcome measure, variance differences (H3) and growth-curve shapes (H4) are artefacts of measurement non-equivalence, not governance effects._
**3.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _The frame assumes participant-level decay (H1, H2) is the relevant unit, but a comparison-case reframing asks: in analogous domains where structural reinforcement was absent, did system-level outcomes persist even when individual-level measures decayed — suggesting the individual is the wrong unit of analysis entirely?_
**4.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _H1 through H4 are all posed as main-effect or two-way interaction hypotheses; a structural decomposition outside this frame asks whether a threshold or phase-transition model — rather than a linear or compounding growth model — better describes the T×G outcome space, making the current hypotheses a special case of a broader non-linear landscape._
**5.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _Actor-mix consideration: the hypotheses treat implementers and participants as passive recipients of governance and reversibility conditions, but if implementers actively renegotiate governance mode mid-deployment (e.g., G1 drifting toward G2), the between-condition boundaries assumed by H3 and H4 dissolve, and the frame must be replaced with one that models governance as an endogenous time-varying covariate._
**6.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _Reframing the question itself: all four hypotheses presuppose that effect size is the appropriate success criterion, but an outside-frame move asks whether effect-size maximisation is the right objective function — if resilience under perturbation or equitable distribution across sites is the goal, the entire hypothesis ranking changes without any of H1–H4 being empirically wrong._
**7.** [outside-original-frame] 
   _Comparison-case reframing: H2's reversibility logic (mental discounting of undoable commitments) maps onto behavioural economics findings on option value; importing that literature reframes H2 not as a novel prediction but as a special case of known option-value theory, shifting the scientific contribution question from 'is H2 true?' to 'does this domain boundary-test the option-value model?' — a different research design follows._

## Territory 4 — Unresolved Uncertainty (6 entries — Step 9)

- OOF-05 governance-drift operationalization: it remains unresolved whether 'governance-drifting' can be reliably detected via deployment logs in real time or only retrospectively, which would materially change whether quarantine at A1 is prospectively enforceable or merely a post-hoc exclusion rule.
- OOF-01 political-economy pre-selection: the degree to which institutional funding rules and regulatory regimes constrain the available T×R×G configuration space to a non-representative subset is unknown prior to the A1 audit, and if severe, the entire confirmatory design may have insufficient configuration coverage to test H1–H4 with external validity.
- OOF-02 measurement invariance achievability: it is unresolved whether any instrument revision triggered by scalar invariance failure can realistically achieve the required threshold within the planned timeline, potentially indefinitely blocking H3 and H4 from the testing queue.
- OOF-03 comparison-case availability: it is unresolved whether ≥2 analogous cases with both individual-level and system-level outcome time series exist and are accessible, which would make the U3 cross-level correlation analysis infeasible and leave the unit-of-analysis question open.
- OOF-04 pilot data adequacy for non-linearity scan: it is unresolved whether available pilot or archival data have sufficient temporal resolution and sample size to distinguish a threshold/phase-transition model from a linear/compounding model at the pre-registered ΔAIC or ΔBIC threshold, potentially forcing N4 to proceed on underpowered evidence.
- OOF-06/OOF-07 stakeholder objective-function consensus: it is unresolved whether the O5 structured review will produce a single adoptable primary objective function or deadlock among competing stakeholder preferences, which would prevent closure of the pre-registration document and block confirmatory data collection indefinitely.
