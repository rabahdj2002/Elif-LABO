## Territory 1 — Decomposition Axes (Step 2)

**Axis 1: Therapeutic Access vs. Autonomy Integrity**
  _The frame's central tension is whether expanding mental-health reach via AI infrastructure can be decoupled from the erosion of individual psychological autonomy, relational agency, and dignity—making this axis the primary load-bearing fault line._
    - **A1**: Access-maximization: AI deployment closes the therapist-gap and reaches underserved, help-resistant, or geographically isolated populations
    - **A2**: Dependency risk: prolonged AI-mediated emotional support restructures relational capacity and normalizes machine-mediated development across generations
    - **A3**: Substitution vs. augmentation: whether AI acts as a stopgap scaffold for human care or permanently displaces it as the primary therapeutic modality

**Axis 2: Surveillance Scope vs. Governance Containment**
  _The integration of biometric, behavioral, linguistic, and social-isolation data creates a population-scale psychological surveillance substrate whose risks are structurally separable from the therapeutic rationale and require their own governance axis._
    - **B1**: Data collection perimeter: which signal types (wearable, voice, smartphone, sleep) are permissible inputs and under what consent architecture
    - **B2**: Institutional containment: hard prohibitions against merger of emotional-profile data with insurance, employment, education, or law-enforcement systems
    - **B3**: Power concentration risk: state and corporate actors acquiring unprecedented psychological influence over populations through infrastructure ownership

**Axis 3: Metric Optimization vs. Human Flourishing**
  _The frame surfaces a structural risk that optimization targets (behavioral stability, adherence, relapse scores) are proxy metrics that may systematically diverge from genuine flourishing, making this axis load-bearing for any evaluation of deployment conditions or red lines._
    - **C1**: Stability-optimization: systems trained to minimize crisis events, maximize adherence, and reduce flagged-risk scores as primary success criteria
    - **C2**: Flourishing-orientation: evaluation frameworks that center subjective wellbeing, relational depth, self-determination, and long-run psychological development
    - **C3**: Misclassification harm: predictive labeling of vulnerable individuals generating stigma, coercive intervention, or systemic disadvantage via false-positive risk scores

## Territory 2 — Multi-scale Relations (Step 6)

**Scales:**

- individual (patient/user autonomy, therapeutic access, personal metric optimization)
- institutional (clinical governance, surveillance program design, organizational flourishing metrics)
- systemic/field (healthcare-system norms, data-infrastructure standards, regulatory frameworks)
- civilizational (long-run human flourishing, epistemic autonomy at population scale, entrenchment of surveillance as default)

**Cross-scale relations:**

- Therapeutic access optimized at the individual scale (A1–A3) does not aggregate to autonomy integrity at the systemic/field scale: population-wide clinical interventions can normalize dependency or erode informed-consent norms even when each individual encounter appears voluntary.
- Surveillance scope decisions made at the institutional scale (B1–B3) operate on a slower revision timescale than the individual-scale harms they produce: by the time governance containment mechanisms catch up, surveillance infrastructure is already entrenched and technically costly to reverse.
- Metric optimization legible at the institutional scale (C2) obscures human-flourishing signals that only become visible at the civilizational scale (C3): short-cycle performance indicators suppress long-horizon, qualitative outcomes that no single institution has the mandate or lifespan to track.
- Governance containment frameworks assembled at the systemic/field scale (B3) constrain, but do not determine, the surveillance-scope choices available at the institutional scale (B1–B2): field-level norms set a ceiling on permissible data collection, yet institutional actors retain discretionary headroom that accumulates into civilizational-scale epistemic risk.
- Autonomy-integrity protections enacted at the individual scale (A2–A3) are necessary but not sufficient conditions for autonomy integrity at the civilizational scale: individual opt-out mechanisms do not aggregate to population-level resistance when structural incentives uniformly favor therapeutic-access expansion.
