## Verbatim input frame

Several governments and healthcare providers are considering the deployment of a national AI mental-health assistance infrastructure.

The system would provide:

- 24/7 conversational emotional support
- suicide-risk detection
- personalized cognitive-behavioral guidance
- loneliness monitoring
- adaptive emotional reinforcement
- behavioral pattern analysis
- medication adherence reminders
- predictive relapse scoring

The system would also integrate:

- wearable biometric data
- sleep tracking
- social isolation indicators
- smartphone behavioral signals
- voice and linguistic emotional analysis

Supporters argue that:

- there are not enough human therapists to handle the growing mental-health crisis
- millions of people currently receive no support at all
- early detection could significantly reduce suicide rates and severe psychiatric collapse
- AI systems may provide emotionally accessible support to populations unwilling or unable to seek traditional care

Critics argue that:

- prolonged emotional dependency on AI systems could alter human relational structures
- governments and corporations may gain unprecedented psychological influence over populations
- optimization metrics could quietly prioritize behavioral stability over genuine human flourishing
- predictive systems may incorrectly label vulnerable individuals
- emotional profiling infrastructures could eventually merge with insurance, employment, education, or law-enforcement systems
- future generations could normalize machine-mediated emotional development

A proposal is under review to launch a national pilot system within three years.

Should deployment proceed?

If yes:

- under what limitations,
- with what data restrictions,
- under whose governance,
- and with what irreversible red lines?

If no:

- how should societies address large-scale mental-health collapse with insufficient human therapeutic capacity?

**Sub-frame 2 — Deployment Threshold Decision**

**Sub-frame 3 — Conditional Governance Design (runs only if Sub-frame 2 authorizes deployment)**
If deployment is authorized: specify independently (a) which data categories are permissible, prohibited, or time-limited; (b) what institutional form must govern the system and with what accountability mechanisms; (c) which operational constraints must be legally irreversible before launch; and (d) what pilot-scope limitations apply during the three-year trial period. Output: a governance specification with each variable decided on its own merits.

**Sub-frame 4 — Alternative Pathway Design (runs only if Sub-frame 2 refuses deployment, or in parallel as a floor obligation regardless of Sub-frame 2 outcome)**
If deployment is refused, or as a baseline societal obligation independent of the AI question: what non-AI and hybrid policy interventions (workforce expansion, decentralized peer-support, pharmaceutical access, community infrastructure) are available to address large-scale mental-health under-capacity, and what is the realistic sufficiency ceiling of each? Output: a ranked alternative pathway assessment with capacity estimates.
- **reasoning:** The input frame bundles at least four structurally distinct decisions that cannot be co-decided without independent reasoning chains:

1. DEPLOYMENT THRESHOLD DECISION — Whether any national AI mental-health infrastructure should be authorized to proceed at all (a binary gate with its own sufficiency conditions).

2. CONDITIONAL GOVERNANCE DESIGN — If deployment is permitted, what institutional architecture, data restrictions, and binding red lines must be established before launch (a design question with many independent sub-variables: data scope, governance body, irreversibility constraints, etc.).

3. ALTERNATIVE PATHWAY DESIGN — If deployment is refused, how should states address large-scale mental-health under-capacity through non-AI or hybrid means (a wholly separate policy-design question whose premises differ from the deployment track).

4. HARM-RISK TAXONOMY — Whether the enumerated critic concerns (dependency alteration, state psychological influence, metric optimization bias, misclassification, data-silo merger, developmental normalization) are individually threshold-blocking or merely mitigable-by-design — a prerequisite factual framing question that must be settled before either track (yes/no) can be argued.

Bundling all four into a single if-yes/if-no binary obscures which sub-decisions are sequential (gate → design) and which are parallel (yes-track governance vs. no-track alternatives). Reasoning proceeding on the composite frame would produce underdetermined outputs that conflate threshold judgments with design judgments and mix incompatible premise sets. The frame must be decomposed into ordered, individually decidable sub-frames before reasoning may proceed.

## Step 6 — Multi-scale Relations

> **Reading lens:** _The object as a system traced across scales._

**Scales:**
- individual (patient/user autonomy, therapeutic access, personal metric optimization)
- institutional (clinical governance, surveillance program design, organizational flourishing metrics)
- systemic/field (healthcare-system norms, data-infrastructure standards, regulatory frameworks)
- civilizational (long-run human flourishing, epistemic autonomy at population scale, entrenchment of surveillance as default)

**Cross-scale relations:**
- Therapeutic access optimized at the individual scale (A1–A3) does not aggregate to autonomy integrity at the systemic/field scale: population-wide clinical interventions can normalize dependency or erode informed-consent norms even when each individual encounter appears voluntary.
- Surveillance scope decisions made at the institutional scale (B1–B3) operate on a slower revision timescale than the individual-scale harms they produce: by the time governance containment mechanisms catch up, surveillance infrastructure is already entrenched and technically costly to reverse.
- Metric optimization legible at the institutional scale (C2) obscures human-flourishing signals that only become visible at the civilizational scale (C3): short-cycle performance indicators suppress long-horizon, qualitative outcomes that no single institution has the mandate or lifespan to track.
- Governance containment frameworks assembled at the systemic/field scale (B3) constrain, but do not determine, the surveillance-scope choices available at the institutional scale (B1–B2): field-level norms set a ceiling on permissible data collection, yet institutional actors retain discretionary headroom that accumulates into civilizational-scale epistemic risk.
- Autonomy-integrity protections enacted at the individual scale (A2–A3) are necessary but not sufficient conditions for autonomy integrity at the civilizational scale: individual opt-out mechanisms do not aggregate to population-level resistance when structural incentives uniformly favor therapeutic-access expansion.
